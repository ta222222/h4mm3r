The "Repost" Economy

Wordshammer staff
Issue02 / Summer
Read16 Min
FiledImage / Net

Every screenshot you have ever loved is already a ghost. The poor image does not arrive — it accumulates, like sediment in a group chat nobody remembers starting.

I first understood this watching a friend repost a JPEG of a Y2K-era Paris Hilton paparazzi still — third generation, colour-blown, crowned with compression artefacts like a halo of digital rust. She captioned it "mood." The image had no author, no date, no resolution worth naming. It was, in Hito Steyerl's phrase, an illicit bastard of an original image. And it was perfect.

The poor image is not a failure of technology. It is a social form — passed on as lure, decoy, index, reminder of a former visual self. On Tumblr in 2011, on Instagram in 2019, on TikTok last Tuesday, the same degraded file circulates with new irony layered over old sincerity. Each repost adds a stratum: nostalgia, camp, critique, grief. The file gets worse. The meaning gets richer. This is not loss. This is methodology.

At Art Center, we were taught to worship clarity — the crisp render, the perfect print, the portfolio-ready documentation. But the images that actually moved through our studios were never those. They were screencaps of screencaps. Phone photos of laptop screens. PDFs of essays forwarded without the cover page. Visual research that arrived already tired, already translated, already someone else's memory of someone else's reference.

Steyerl wrote that the poor image mocks the promises of digital technology. I think it mocks something else too: the fantasy that an image ever belonged to anyone. Ownership assumes a stable original. The poor image refuses that contract entirely. Its genealogy is dubious. Its filenames are deliberately misspelled. It defies patrimony, national culture, copyright. It is passed hand to hand like contraband — or like love notes in a classroom where nobody is paying attention to the lesson.

"It mocks the promises of digital technology. Not only is it often degraded to the point of being just a hurried blur, one even doubts whether it could be called an image at all."
— Hito Steyerl, In Defence of the Poor Image, 2009

Consider the fashion campaign that only exists as a cropped repost. Consider the art installation documented through a visitor's flash photograph, re-uploaded at 480p, saved again as a WhatsApp forward. By the time it reaches you, the work is gone — replaced by an atmosphere, a colour temperature, a vibe. Luxury brands understood this early. Louis Vuitton does not need you to see the bag clearly. They need you to feel the blur: speed, exclusivity, the impossibility of full resolution when desire moves faster than bandwidth.

The poor image is the native visual language of that economy. High fashion and low file size. Couture and compression. The same contradiction Dazed has been printing for decades — immaculate taste delivered through imperfect means.

"Still posting about the same night like it was Coachella. It was a Tuesday. In February."
— hammer staff, unusually calm

Nowhere is the repost economy more elegantly bankrupt than the modern DJ Instagram. They do not post — they repost. Your photo. Your story. Your fifteen-second clip of the booth from an angle they would never have gotten themselves, now sitting on their grid with a fire emoji and "what a night" as if they authored the light.

This is not collaboration. This is curatorial shoplifting with a tag. One set becomes a content franchise: the same bass drop clipped seventeen ways, the same crowd shot reframed as "energy," the same afters mirror selfie resurfacing in August like a seasonal collection drop. Spring/Summer: warehouse. Resort: warehouse again. Pre-Fall: still warehouse. Couture week: a ten-second snippet from a party three months ago, captioned "grateful." Grateful for what — the algorithm? The friend who filmed it for free?

Watch a recap story after any headline set and count the sources. Six reposts. Zero original files. Everyone tagged except the person who actually held the phone. The night achieves immortality not as memory but as inventory — passed between accounts like a sample pack nobody licensed, looped until the compression artefacts become the brand.

We are not saying do not go to the club. We are saying we have seen that story. We saw it when Felix posted it. We saw it when you reposted Felix. We will see it again in October when someone needs to fill a quiet Thursday. The poor image, here, is not theory. It is a business model: maximum atmosphere, minimum new material, infinite second-hand nostalgia for a party that ended before your Uber arrived.

032c runs on this logic too — the editorial spread as archive fever, images pulled from personal collections and resequenced until they mean something new. Not illustration. Curation as authorship. The poor image asks: what if curation is all there ever was? What if the original was always a fiction we agreed to perform?

I keep a folder on my desktop called RESEARCH that is mostly other people's screenshots. Sophie Calle's Take Care of Yourself beside a pixelated still from Dogtooth beside a photo of a park bench plaque I never visited. None of these files are mine. All of them are mine. They form a canon without permission — the way a magazine issue forms a canon, the way a group chat forms a canon, the way heartbreak forms one.

Hammer's research board understands this intuitively. Drop a link. Drop a file. Drag to rearrange. The board does not ask for provenance. It asks for proximity — what sits next to what, what rhymes across distance, what compression pattern repeats. This is editing. This is criticism. This is the work.

There is a politics here, and Steyerl named it: the poor image is often the visual form of the dispossessed, the migrant, the amateur. But there is also a pleasure — the pleasure of the repost, the inside joke, the file so degraded it becomes abstract, becomes art, becomes yours because nobody else would want it in that condition.

Post-internet humour runs on this. The 2011 meme revived at the wrong resolution. The Britney blackout era JPEG treated like a religious icon. The hauntological thumbnail — Fisher's lost futures compressed into a YouTube preview frame that never loads fully. We are not looking at images anymore. We are looking at the residue of looking.

"We are not looking at images anymore. We are looking at the residue of looking."
— this dispatch, generation unknown

So what does a magazine do with the poor image? Not restore it. Not apologise for it. Publish it as found — with the blur intact, the metadata stripped, the generation count unknowable. Let the layout do what the file cannot: give it sequence, give it breath, give it a body on the page even when the pixels refuse to cohere.

This dispatch is that attempt. Every image here arrived tired. Every sentence tries to catch up. If you want the high-resolution version of this argument, it does not exist. It never did. Take care of yourself.
