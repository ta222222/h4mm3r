Do Influencers Still Exist?

Wordshammer staff
Issue02 / Culture
Read14 Min
FiledInstagram / AI / Fashion

The influencer is dead. Not literally — there are still people with ring lights and affiliate codes — but the category has collapsed into something else: a swarm of identical faces, AI-generated girls in borrowed Margiela, and fashion people performing authenticity so hard the performance became the product.

Ask anyone under thirty if they follow influencers and they will laugh. They follow people, they say, or accounts, or nothing at all because the feed is broken. The word influencer sounds like 2017 — like brunch content and flat-lay skincare and a time when posting could still feel like a career path instead of a symptom. Now it is ambient. Everyone is one. No one admits it.

Instagram did this. It built a machine that rewarded sameness — same angles, same captions, same beige apartment, same "day in my life" cadence — until originality became a liability. The algorithm does not want your weirdness. It wants your face in the format it already understands. Fashion absorbed the logic wholesale: micro-trends that expire in six weeks, aesthetics named after TikTok sounds, entire subcultures reduced to a carousel of reference photos everyone already saved.

Then AI arrived and made the corpse twitch. Virtual models with impossible cheekbones wear real clothes from real brands. Agencies list them beside human talent. A generated girl in a vintage poncho gets more engagement than the stylist who actually owns the poncho. The comment section asks who is she and nobody has an answer that satisfies — because the answer is: she is everyone and no one, a composite of every face the platform already liked.

"We stopped asking who made the image and started asking who made it first — as if that still mattered."
— hammer staff, deleted thread

In fashion especially, the copy loop is total. A look walks a runway in Milan. By the time the livestream ends, three mood boards exist. By morning, a Depop seller has tagged the silhouette. By afternoon, a creator in Shoreditch is wearing a thrifted approximation for a mirror selfie with the caption obsessed. By evening, a brand reposts the selfie as "community love." The original designer's name is optional. Provenance is a luxury nobody can afford at scroll speed.

Art used to be the escape hatch — the place where copying was discussed, where citation was ritual, where stealing well was a skill. Now art accounts on Instagram look like fashion accounts. Same grid rhythm. Same serif caption font. Same "process video" that ends before anything difficult happens. The gallery is a content studio. The studio is a showroom. Everyone is curating a personal brand, even the people who claim to hate personal brands, especially them.

People copy each other because copying is the only language left. Not homage — not even theft with flair — just parallel production. Two photographers shoot the same model in the same doorway on the same afternoon and both posts go up within an hour. Neither is copying the other on purpose. They are copying the same mood board, the same Pinterest ghost, the same algorithmic suggestion that said: this is what works now.

The old influencer economy depended on trust — parasocial, fragile, monetizable. You believed she actually used the serum. You believed his fit was spontaneous. That trust is gone, replaced by a low-grade suspicion that attaches to everything: Is this sponsored? Is this AI? Is this even a person? Brands respond by paying more for "authenticity," which produces content that looks less authentic than ever — lo-fi on purpose, messy apartment on purpose, crying in the car on purpose, all of it staged with the precision of a Super Bowl ad.

So do influencers still exist? Only if you stretch the definition until it means nothing. There are still accounts with millions of followers. There are still gifted packages and discount codes and "link in bio." But the figure of the influencer — the singular tastemaker who could move product with a smile — has been replaced by a distributed chorus of imitators, some human, some not, all performing the same hunger for relevance in a feed that no longer rewards being first, only being familiar.

What survives is not influence but inventory. Bodies as inventory. Outfits as inventory. Opinions as inventory, refreshed weekly. Fashion weeks become content weeks. Front rows are lighting setups. The show is the B-roll. Designers complain that nobody watches the clothes anymore, then hire the same creators who never look at the clothes to stand outside in them anyway.

Maybe that is the obituary. Not that influence ended, but that it became too cheap to measure. When everyone can look like an influencer, being an influencer stops meaning you matter. It means you showed up. You posted. You wore the boot. You tagged the brand. You copied the pose from someone who copied it from a campaign from two seasons ago that copied a nineties editorial nobody remembered until the repost account found it.

The question is not whether influencers still exist. The question is whether we still need the word — or whether we have all already become the thing it used to describe, and the only difference now is that none of us get paid enough to pretend otherwise.
