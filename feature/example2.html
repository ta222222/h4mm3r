Someone Else's Summer

WordsCleo Park
Issue02 / Summer
Read9 Min
FiledParty / Music

Sometimes the night is very long, and the speaker feels too small. Even when, in other instances, the speaker was too big, and the block too quiet — flat, empty, waiting for bass.

I found the playlist by accident, the way you find most things that end up mattering. A link in a group chat from someone who had already left the thread — rooftop at nine, BYOB, dress code: hot. No address, just a cross street and a pin that moved twice before lock-in. I saved it without thinking. Phone out, tap, added. Later I couldn't remember who sent it. The file became the party. This is how most summers work now: not at the door, but in the camera roll, filed beside voice memos and screenshots of songs Shazam'd through an open window.

Public parties pretend to be spontaneous. They are not. They are group texts, and borrowed speakers, and the slow generosity of strangers dancing three feet from someone they will never see again. I photographed the strobe without thinking — flash on, click, saved. The image became the invite. This is how most joy works now: not at the site, but in the archive, sitting next to set lists and JPEGs of JPEGs of flyers.

Every city has a summer canon — the songs that return when the heat breaks 90 and the windows stay open. Britney through a car door. Glass through headphones at the pool. A DJ set you only catch the last twenty minutes of because you were late finding parking. My playlist is the opposite of official: private language made public again, a stranger's taste entering my rotation without permission, sitting next to field recordings and film stills and photos of mickey mouse sunglasses at a swap meet.

I think this is sweet, and real. Devotion does not require an RSVP. You can love a night you never fully attended. You can keep a song because someone else played it once at the right hour. The party becomes a vessel — not for the perfect memory, exactly, but for the living who need a beat to aim their feeling when the feeling has no proper address.

"Turn it up — they're playing our song."
— overheard on the stairs, every summer

At Art Center we talked about scale constantly — the scale of the canvas, the scale of the ambition, the scale of the loan you took to be there. Nobody talked about the scale of the night. How music expands to fill whatever container you give it. How a backyard can hold more than a gallery if the playlist is right.

There is a whole literature of the afterparty — the hour when the DJ gets brave, when the tempo loosens, when someone pulls out a phone and everyone pretends not to notice the flash. The summer mix is hauntology in MP3. It keeps playing after the host goes to bed. It keeps playing after the listeners leave. It keeps playing for me, a person who was not on the original guest list, which might be the most honest form of audience there is.

There is a politics of public sound — who gets the permit, who gets the subwoofer, who gets shushed when the neighbors call. But there is also something smaller and more radical: the anonymous playlist as found object, as readymade, as ready-made-feeling. You press play. You dance. You turn it up, or you don't. The speaker does not judge.

I keep returning to the link on my phone. Not because it is beautiful — it isn't. Because it is sufficient. Because the night is long and the speaker is always the wrong size and still we show up. Still we move. Still we save the file, drag it onto the board, let it sit next to everything else we couldn't stop replaying.

Cleo Park Essayist and contributor. Her writing moves between nightlife, summer playlists, and the songs we keep on repeat without knowing why. Based in Los Angeles.
