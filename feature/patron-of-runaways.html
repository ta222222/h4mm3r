Patron Of Runaways

The saint of mental health, runaways, and unknowable causes.

A goth journalist is preparing this piece for Hammer Issue 03. The subject is not a person but a category — saints filed under the wrong spelling, runaways who never asked to be icons, mental health rendered as folklore instead of a pamphlet.

Every runaway carries a patron they did not choose. Sometimes it is a saint with a name misspelled on a prayer card. Sometimes it is a pop figure pressed into service because the official roster felt too clean. The journalist keeps a list of these substitutions — who people pray to when the licensed saints feel like HR.

Mental health, in this draft, arrives as weather. Low pressure before the bus out of town. A ceiling fan that clicks like a metronome for intrusive thoughts. The piece refuses the infographic — no numbered steps, no pastel icons — only testimony and bad lighting.

The patron of runaways is said to protect those who leave without a map. Historians argue the name. Runaways argue the feeling. Both agree the icon should look tired, not triumphant.

Issue 03 will ask who gets to be holy when the exit was not graceful. Subway platforms. Highway shoulders. A cousin's couch that becomes a calendar. The journalist interviews no one yet — only accumulates fragments: a bracelet, a bus ticket stub, a saint medal bought at a gas station because the face looked kind.

Unknowable causes are the honest ones. You do not need to explain why you left if the leaving itself is the argument. The patron listens without requiring a thesis. That is the fantasy. That is also the workload no saint was ever meant to carry.

Goth journalism, in this version, means writing from the aisle seat at 3am. It means black ink on newsprint that smears when you cry, which you are not supposed to admit in copy but will anyway. It means the Hammer logo centered above the text like a seal on a letter you were afraid to send.

The draft keeps growing because the subject refuses a conclusion. Runaways do not end — they reroute. Patron saints get reassigned. Mental health shifts shape when the season changes and the basement gets too hot or too cold.

There will be a section on misspellings — how devotion survives error. How a name written wrong on a wall can still function as a door. How the journalist's own notes say runways when they mean runaways and whether that slip is part of the story or a joke the patron is telling back.

There will be a section on music, probably — something minor key, something played too loud in a room where nobody asks you to explain yourself. The Hammer piece is not a playlist, but it understands that runaways often travel with one song looping like a spell.

Until the guest writer signs, this page stays a threshold. Scroll it. Watch the logo find center. That movement is also part of the draft — the magazine insisting on its name while the story underneath still refuses to land.
